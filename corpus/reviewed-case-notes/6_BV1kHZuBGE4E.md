## Calculation Logic

- If current revenue is about 1,700/day and true contribution margin is uncertain, the first task is not marketing; it is finding actual gross profit per product.
- Known daily fixed burden is roughly rent 277 + counted labor 333 + utilities 140 = about 750/day before other costs.
- At 40% gross margin, 1,700/day gives 680 gross profit, barely near fixed burden. If true margin is lower after discounts, platform settlement, oil, packaging, waste, and headquarters material pricing, the store immediately loses money.
- The transcript's own calculation lands at about 394/day loss, showing that the owner's margin assumptions are not reliable enough for decision-making.

## Diagnosis

The fatal problem is not simply "business is slow." It is a confused model:

- The owner cannot calculate gross margin.
- The menu is too broad for a small owner-operated first store.
- The store is not really a hamburger shop; it is a headquarters-supplied equipment/materials bundle.
- Weekday demand does not match the menu complexity.
- Labor is too high for current revenue.
- Debt pressure makes slow experimentation dangerous.

## Recommendation Pattern

- Do not chase "increase revenue" before calculating product gross margin.
- Cut labor first. If the owner couple can run the shop and revenue is around 1,000-1,700/day, extra staff destroy the model.
- Drop the broad franchise menu. Pick one local just-demand category: rice noodles, wontons/dumplings, rice rolls/noodles, or a simple snack add-on around family and student flow.
- Remove low-margin pizza/hamburger/milk-tea clutter unless the product's real margin and demand are proven.
- Ask headquarters for refund or partial return of franchise-related fees/material burden, while preserving payment records and chat promises.
- Use weekend part-time labor rather than full-week fixed labor if the store only has weekend traffic.

## Reusable Heuristics

- "我毛利不知道" is an emergency stop signal. 勇哥's first repair is to make the owner calculate each product.
- A franchise that sells too many categories often makes money from equipment and materials, not from the store's operating model.
- If the user is already in debt, avoid advice that requires more spending before fixing margin, labor, and category focus.
- Small community/plaza stores should usually win with one clear just-demand product, not a complete restaurant menu.
